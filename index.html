Citing Catherine Minter from her 2013 article "Academic Library Reform and the Ideal of the Librarian in England, France,and
Germany in the Long Nineteenth Century":
At the end of the eighteenth century, academic and research libraries throughout Western Europe were often poorly stocked because they had no steady fund for book purchases. In Great Britain, the Bodleian was deeply deficient in terms of its holdings, even after almost two centuries of existence. It was not until 1780 that an annual fee of four shillings was imposed on all persons entitled to read in the Library and on all who had exceeded four years from matriculation, and the Library was assigned a share of matriculation fees, in an effort to remedy its acknowledged shortcomings.
